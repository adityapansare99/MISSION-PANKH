Sleep Cycles

Sleep Duration:

Insufficient sleep or chronic sleep deprivation can affect blood sugar control. Research suggests that inadequate sleep duration may lead to insulin resistance, a condition in which the body becomes less responsive to insulin, resulting in higher blood sugar levels. It can also disrupt the balance of hormones involved in glucose regulation, such as cortisol and growth hormone.

Sleep Quality:

The quality of sleep is also important. Disrupted or poor-quality sleep, such as frequent awakenings or sleep apnea, can contribute to insulin resistance and higher blood sugar levels. Sleep apnea, a condition characterised by pauses in breathing during sleep, has been associated with higher rates of insulin resistance and an increased risk of type 2 diabetes.

Prioritise Sleep:

Aim for 7-9 hours of quality sleep per night to support overall health, including blood sugar control.Make your bedroom conducive to sleep by ensuring a comfortable temperature, minimizing noise and light, and establishing a relaxing bedtime routine.It’s important to note that sleep alone may not completely control blood sugar levels in individuals with diabetes or prediabetes. A comprehensive approach that includes a healthy diet, regular physical activity, proper medication management, and regular monitoring of blood sugar levels is essential.

Establish a Consistent Sleep Schedule:

Going to bed and waking up at consistent times can help regulate your body’s internal clock and promote better blood sugar control.Stress can affect sleep quality and blood sugar levels. Incorporating stress management techniques, such as relaxation exercises, mindfulness, or engaging in enjoyable activities, can help reduce stress levels and promote better sleep.
